The storm had been building since early afternoon, that particular bruised greenish light that rolls in off the Gulf of St. Lawrence when it means it. Lucas had watched it from the ridge above the Hamel valley, eating the last of his lunch crackers and trying to convince himself it would pass north. It did not pass north. By four o'clock the sky was the colour of a week-old bruise and the wind had reversed itself twice, which is the kind of thing that earns your attention in Newfoundland even if you've never hiked a day in your life.

He had been on the Long Range Traverse for two days. He'd done his research — most people do their research — which mostly amounted to a thorough understanding of how unprepared research leaves you. The traverse ran thirty-eight kilometers through some of the emptiest terrain on the island, a high plateau of exposed rock and tuckamore and bogs that had no bottom you could trust, and the designated campsites were spaced with the cheerful optimism of people who planned their days around weather that cooperated. He was still four kilometers short of the second site when the sky opened.

Not a Newfoundland drizzle. Not the soft grey mist that the province wore like a coat most of the year. This was a full commitment — horizontal rain that stung exposed skin and turned the already-sodden ground into something that wanted to keep your boots. Within ten minutes he was wet in the way where wet stops being a condition and becomes just the new state of your body. He pulled his pack cover and kept moving, head down, calculating.

The designated site was still a hard two hours away. Possibly three in this. The plateau offered nothing — no trees above knee height, no rock faces, nothing but open exposure and the wind scouring across it hard enough to make his pack a sail against him. He needed lower ground. He cut east off the main route, following the fall line down off the plateau into the tree zone, telling himself he'd circle back to the trail once things settled.

The trees thickened fast below the treeline — black spruce and fir twisted into each other in the way that tuckamore grows, low and dense and interlocked. He pushed through a section of it for twenty minutes before the land opened slightly into a narrow depression, and that was where he saw it.

A structure. Not much of one, but a structure.

He stood at the edge of the treeline and looked at it for a moment with the rain coming hard off the back of his neck. It was small — maybe four meters by five, built from cut timber that had gone deeply grey with age, the roof pitched low and covered with what might have once been shingles but were now just suggestions of shingles, a patchwork of moss and rot and a few bare wood strips. One of the two visible windows was boarded. The other was open to the weather, the glass long gone. A door, still on its hinges, had swung almost fully closed. He could not imagine what had brought someone to build anything out here, this far from any road, this deep in terrain that the map marked as simply empty. Some old lumber survey camp, maybe. A trapper's cabin from before his father's time.
He crossed to it in a half-jog, shoulder into the wind.

The door swung inward with a sound that was more compression than creak, the wood soft enough that the hinges had settled into it. Inside was dim and smelled immediately of something old — not just age, but specifically the smell of things that had been damp long enough to begin the long conversion from one thing into another. Mulch. Cellar. The back of a drawer that's been closed for decades. He stood in the doorway and let his eyes adjust.

One room. A wood stove in the corner with its pipe gone, the hole in the roof above it covered with a square of plywood that had buckled inward. A shelf along one wall with nothing on it. The floor was board, original lumber, and when he stepped inside he felt the boards give slightly underfoot in the way that boards give when the joists beneath them have begun to lose the argument with moisture. He tested three spots before he found purchase near the stove corner, where the floor held solid.

He would not call it good shelter. He would call it better than the plateau.

He pushed the door closed behind him and stood in the near-dark listening to the rain on the roof, which sounded like it hit in most places and found its way through in a few. He could see the slow drip from a corner where the roof had separated from the wall, a dark stain that had been spreading long enough to have grown something soft and dark over the lower boards. The plywood over the stove pipe hole dripped steadily into the cold stove box. The smell was pervasive, that specific damp rot that worked into the back of your sinuses.

But the walls held. The floor held in the places he tested. The wind, which outside had been deafening, became here only a pressure, the whole structure flexing slightly but not in a way that worried him, the flex of something that had outlasted a lot of storms.

He set up his pad against the back wall, the furthest from the leaking corner, and got his bivy out. Changed his shirt in the cold. He had a small camp stove, a pocket rocket type, and he lit it in the corner near the stove — brick and old iron, at least nothing flammable — and set water boiling for a meal. A pouch of chicken and rice that had been in his pack since day one. He hadn't been particularly hungry before he lit the stove, but the smell of the water heating and the small bright circle of the stove flame made something in him settle.

The storm got worse before it got better. By six o'clock it had worked itself up to something that rattled the boarded window in its frame and drove rain in thin streams through the gap between the door and its jamb. He'd stuffed his rain jacket against the gap and that helped. He ate the chicken and rice without tasting much of it, sitting with his knees up and his headlamp on its lowest setting, and tried to read something on his phone before the thought of the battery dying made him put it away.

He noticed the floor near the far wall when he got up to piss in the corner where the drip had already ruined the boards — no sense making another wet spot. He'd crossed that section of floor without thinking about it, and something crumbled under the heel of his boot. Not a splinter. Not loose grit. He put his light on it and saw that the board had simply come apart where he'd stepped, a dry brown sponge where solid wood should have been, the rot so complete it had taken on the consistency of old cork. He pressed a finger to it and a section the size of his palm compressed down and broke, revealing a darkness below and the smell of turned earth.

He moved back to his sleeping area and didn't think much more about it.

He fell asleep in short doses, the way you do in unfamiliar places with weather working on the structure you're inside. He woke at some point and heard the rain still going, still driving hard, and became aware in the slow surfacing way of waking that the smell had gotten worse. More present. He'd been breathing it long enough that it had stopped registering as smell and started registering as taste. Something overripe and faintly sweet, the way apples smell after the first frost finds them.

He rolled over and put his light on the wall beside his head.

The wall had darkened in the two feet above the floor, a black bloom that he was fairly sure hadn't been there when he set up. Or it had been there and he hadn't looked closely enough, because there was nothing subtle about it now — a growth of something soft and dark, the texture of bread mold but darker, in a patch the size of a dinner plate. He watched it the way you watch something you're not sure is moving, the way things seem to move when the light is low and your eyes are tired.

He got up to get his water bottle and his boot went through the floor.

Not far — just the one board, and he caught himself on the adjacent boards before his leg went past the knee. He pulled himself free, heart going, and pointed his light down. Where his boot had punched through, a ragged hole showed nothing below but dark and the smell of raw earth and something else, something sweeter and more insistent. He tested the boards around the hole with his boot before putting weight on them, and two more gave in the same soft, soundless way, compressing inward before breaking, the rot complete and total.

He moved to the stove corner where the floor had held and stayed there.

His throat had started to bother him at some point in the night. He'd been coughing — nothing productive, just a dry catch at the back of the throat, the kind you got with dust or cold air. He drank water and that helped temporarily. He'd been breathing this air for four or five hours now. He opened the door a crack to get fresh air in and the storm pushed back immediately, driving cold rain horizontal through the gap. He held it open for thirty seconds and then closed it. The coughing was nothing. People slept in old buildings all the time.

He was heating water again — the hot drink feeling necessary and specific, something to do with his hands — when the stove slipped. He'd balanced it on a brick he'd found on the floor and it had worked fine the first time, but when he picked up his mug the stove shifted and the pot tipped and the boiling water and the stove went sideways at the same moment, and when he reached to grab the stove the edge of his cook pot caught the knuckle of his middle finger cleanly, the metal seam hot enough and sharp enough to open a cut without much sensation. He pulled his hand back and looked at it in the headlamp.

Not deep. The length of his first knuckle, welling red. He found his first aid kit and taped a piece of gauze over it and pressed until the bleeding slowed.

The coughing woke him again at what his watch said was two in the morning. Worse now, lower in his chest, a productive sound that produced nothing. His nose had started running and when he wiped it with his sleeve there was a dark stain in the fabric that took him a moment to understand. He got his bandana and pinched his nose and tipped his head back and the bleeding stopped after a few minutes, the slow kind of nosebleed that doesn't seem like much until you think about what's causing it.

Something was wrong with the air.

He understood it properly then, the way understanding sometimes waits patiently at the edge of your awareness until you're ready for it. The black growth on the wall had doubled since he'd last looked at it. Or it had always been that size and he'd misread it in the low light. He put his headlamp on full and turned it along the walls and saw it in three other places he hadn't noticed before — the ceiling boards in the center of the room had gone soft and dark, drooping slightly, and where the drip had come down in the corner the wall had taken on a texture that was less wall and more something else, something that moved slightly when the wind pressed the building. He thought of bread, and then stopped thinking of bread.

He stood and assessed what he was actually doing. The rational sequence: pack up, get out, get rain gear on, get into the storm. Walk two kilometers back to the main trail in the dark on flooded ground with his headlamp and the wind pushing him around, and then follow the trail north for another four kilometers in conditions that had a Parks Canada advisory attached to them he'd read before he'd started. The designated campsite or find a boulder overhang and wait it out.

Outside the door, the storm made itself known. A sustained roar, not a gust but a constant pressure, the kind of night that turns exposed terrain into something genuinely hostile.

He sat back down.

The taste in his mouth had changed. Something mineral and deep, underneath everything, the way water from very old pipes tasted, iron and something you couldn't name. He ran his tongue along his teeth and the feeling intensified and he stopped doing that. He drank more water. He tried to eat a granola bar and his stomach rejected the idea completely, a wave of nausea that made him stay very still for a few seconds until it passed.

He coughed for a long time. When he finished he sat in the dark with his forehead on his knees, listening to the building settle around him, the soft concessions it made in the wind. The floor had gone. Not all of it — the stove corner still held — but when he put his light across the room he could see that the boards had sagged between the joists, the whole surface of the floor taking on the appearance of something sunken and soft, as if the earth below it was pulling steadily downward. The hole where his boot had broken through had widened without anything touching it. The wood at its edges was brown-black and peeling back, a slow ongoing collapse that had maybe been in progress for thirty years and was only now finding its conclusion.

He thought, dimly, of the spores. He'd read something once about black mold in flooded houses, the respiratory effects, the way certain fungi reproduced in the air and were breathed into the soft tissue of the lungs. He tried to remember the specific details and couldn't, which was perhaps better. The important thing was that the symptoms — the cough, the nose, the nausea — were consistent with something you could identify and recover from. He was not dying. He was uncomfortable, and he would get out in the morning, and there would be a health center in Rocky Harbour where he could explain the situation and they would understand and it would be fine.

Somewhere around three in the morning he stopped trusting the cough to produce nothing and moved his bivy closer to the door so that if he needed to be sick he could get outside fast. While he was moving it, his knee came down on a section of floor near the stove corner and he felt it give. Not collapse, just give — the soft elastic compression of wood that had no structural integrity left, that was maintaining the appearance of solidity without any of the substance. He moved his weight off it carefully and tested the boards from the edges and there were only three near the stove iron itself that held without flexing.

He was standing on a few square feet of solid floor in the middle of a room that had otherwise become something else.

He put his light on the cut on his hand. He'd been pressing the gauze against it and he pulled it back now to see how it was doing.

The gauze was soaked through, dark brown at the center, and when he peeled it back from the skin he sat very still for a moment, his light steady on his own hand.

The edges of the cut had gone dark. Not bruised dark, not dried blood dark, but the specific black-green of the wall behind him, the colour of the bloom that had started as a dinner plate and was now. He didn't finish that thought. The skin around the cut had gone soft, pulling away from the underlying tissue with a looseness that healthy skin did not have. He touched the edge of it with one finger and felt nothing. Not pain. Nothing.
He understood that he needed to leave.

He got his pack on and his rain gear and he pushed the door open against the storm, and the storm was a wall. Sixty kilometre wind, maybe more, and rain that came at him sideways in the beam of his headlamp, the trees at the edge of the clearing bent hard and the air itself full of horizontal water. He stood in the doorway with one boot on the step and understood what the night would actually ask of him — four kilometers of exposed plateau in this, with visibility down to what his headlamp threw, which was not far, and the ground flooded and the trail marks unreliable in the dark, and his chest already tight and his equilibrium already something he was working to maintain.

He stood there for a long time. Long enough that the rain had soaked his rain jacket to the shoulders and the wind made his eyes water.

He stepped back inside and pulled the door closed.

He sat in the stove corner with his pack on and his rain gear on and his boots on and told himself this was a decision and not a surrender. First light was four hours away. He could hold four hours. He'd done worse than four hours.

The hallucination, if that's what it was, started slow. He had his eyes open and he was watching the floor in the center of the room, the sunken boards, and it seemed to him that the boards were continuing downward, not quickly, but with the patient inevitability of something that had been in progress for a long time. Sinking. The earth below them pulling the rot downward and then pulling the wood, taking it back in the way earth took everything back eventually, the long appetite of the ground for all the things built on top of it. He watched the hole where his boot had gone through and the hole was wider and the darkness below it was a darkness that went further than any crawlspace should, further down than made sense.

He thought about calling out but didn't know who he'd be calling.

He coughed until his eyes watered and his chest felt like something squeezed in a fist.

He must have slept because there was a time when his chin was on his chest and his headlamp had dimmed to almost nothing and the storm had changed pitch outside, a slightly higher sound that he associated, somewhere in the murk of his reasoning, with the quality of storms just before they broke. He lifted his head and put a new battery in his headlamp and the room came back in full.

The floor was gone.

Not the boards — the boards were still there in the same positions, but they had subsided so completely into the earth below them that there was no longer any meaningful distinction between the floor and the ground. The whole surface had become something uniform, black and soft and flat, the wood and the soil and whatever grew between them merged into a single material. Only the few boards in the stove corner where he sat remained elevated, remained discrete. Everything else was part of the same dark plane.

He looked at his hand again because he needed to look at something specific.

He knew, before he unwound the tape, what was under it. Some part of him had known since three in the morning. He pulled the tape back slowly and then the gauze, and the cut on his knuckle had become something else entirely — soft-edged, dark, the skin around it having gone the colour and texture of the walls, the same black-green bloom that had spread from a dinner plate to something he couldn't measure, the same soft rot that had taken the floor and the walls and the boards, and below it, where the cut was deepest, he could see the yellow-white of bone, clean and bright, and the edges of the tissue pulling back from it not from any wound but from the same slow downward appetite that had taken everything else.

He pressed the gauze back against it. Taped it. Sat with both hands in his lap.

Outside, the storm began to ease.

The light came at five-thirty, grey and thin, pressing through the gap in the boarded window. Lucas sat in the stove corner and watched it arrive. The room was very quiet. The rain had dropped to a drizzle. The building settled once, a long slow exhale of its whole frame, and the boards under him shifted slightly and then held.
He got to his feet carefully and moved to the door and opened it.

The treeline was there. The clearing was there, flooded and grey, birch and spruce dripping in the still air. The plateau was somewhere above him. The trail was a few kilometers east. His legs worked, his pack was on, the compass in his chest pocket had north where north should be.

He walked out of the cabin and crossed the clearing without looking back.

The helicopter came in forty minutes, dropping into a clearing above the treeline, and he sat in the back of it with a foil blanket around his shoulders, watching the plateau pass below him, that long grey expanse of exposed rock and bog, and did not look for the depression in the trees where the cabin was. He wasn't sure he could have found it from the air anyway.

In Rocky Harbour a doctor cleaned the cut and said the word necrosis in a neutral tone and wrote things on a pad and told him he was lucky to come in when he did. Lucas said yes. He did not describe the cabin. He didn't know how he would have started.

He drove home to Corner Brook four days later, the hand wrapped and the antibiotics in his bag, the kind of tired that sleep doesn't fully reach. He unlocked his front door and dropped his pack in the hall and stood in his own kitchen for a long moment just letting the ordinary fact of it work on him. The familiar smell of it. The solid floor.

He went to fill a glass of water and noticed, on the windowsill above the sink, a soft dark bloom where the caulking had cracked and let in moisture — mold, ordinary household mold, the kind that appeared every winter when the condensation came. He'd seen it before. He'd meant to deal with it before he left.
He looked at it for a long time. Then pressed one finger to the wood beneath it, gently, the way you'd test unstable ground before putting your weight onto it.

It gave, slightly, under his finger.

He pulled his hand back.

He told himself it was nothing. Old window frame. Rental-grade caulking. He'd deal with it in the morning.

He did not look at it again that night.
