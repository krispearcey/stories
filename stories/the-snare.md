The rabbit had been alive when he’d left it.

Ethan had known that. He'd checked the snare that morning after breakfast, before the frost had fully lifted from the grass, and the animal was there, one hind leg twisted in the wire, its dark eye fixed on nothing. Still breathing. Its sides moving in rapid, shallow pulses; given up, but not expelled from its misery.

His father would have killed it then and there. One clean motion, two fingers behind the skull, a practiced twist. 
<i>You don't let them wait</I>, he'd told Ethan more times than Ethan could count. <i>You don’t let them suffer.</i>

But Ethan’s mind had wandered, not wanting to deal with it right away. The forest he spent countless hours of his childhood in; fighting monsters and playing games, had overgrown with so much new life in the years that he’d been gone. He'd looked at the rabbit once more, told himself it would be fine until he got back, and left.

He hadn’t made it back, and the afternoon had slipped while exploring old memories in a new light. He’d found an old neighbour, and caught up with them over lunch. By the time he was back on the path behind his late father’s cabin, the light had already started its retreat, drawing back from the edges of his surroundings like a tide going out.

Now the guilt sat in his chest like a swallowed stone.

---

His father's oil lamp glowed somewhere ahead through the trees. He could see its faint amber cast shadows between the trunks; but the distance felt wrong. The cabin was maybe a ten minutes' walk from the Corrigans' porch on a bright afternoon. The trees, their shadows, he told himself, just made it seem farther. They always did at dusk.

He cut left off the path to check the snare first. The guilt of letting the poor thing suffer for any longer gnawed at him. The shortcut took him down through a hollow where the ground turned to soft loam and the pines gave way to older trees, their bark dark and deeply grooved, their lower branches hung with a kind of greyish moss he associated with places that didn't get much sun. He'd been through here in daylight before. It had seemed fairly unremarkable then. Now, without the sun, the space between the trunks looked less like gaps and more like absences — holes carved into a lifeless body.

He moved faster.

A branch caught his sleeve. He pulled free without stopping; hearing the seam tear a little as he snarled obscenities in its direction. The ground was uneven, roots bunched up through the soil like fists, and twice he had skipped a step to avoid turning an ankle. His breath came quick and shallow, more from nerves than exertion. He was aware of the silence around him in a way he hadn't been while on the path. It wasn’t true silence, because there were sounds; the slow creak of wooden trunks settling against each other, water cascading down the nearby creek, the occasional flutter of leaves overhead. It was a silence of the kind that meant the normal sounds were absent. No birds. No rustle of small things among the brush.

The forest, for lack of a better word, felt occupied.

He slowed without meaning to. His body making a decision his mind hadn't caught up to yet. He stood a moment, listening to the creek, trying to use it as an anchor, and noticed that it sounded farther away than it had a minute ago. He hadn't changed direction. He was certain of that.

He kept moving.

The hollow deepened as he went, the ground dropping away in gradual steps that he felt more than saw, and the older trees pressed closer together until the last of the remaining light overhead was more of a memory. He found he was watching the spaces between the trunks more than where he was walking. Something in him had decided the spaces were more important. That whatever needed watching was there, in the dark between things, and not on the ground in front of his feet.

It was an old habit. Boyhood logic. He'd spent enough evenings in these woods as a kid to know how the dark played with you, how it took a shape you'd walk past a hundred times in daylight and make it into something it wasn't. The mind filled the gaps. That was all this was.

---

The snare was strung between two saplings about forty metres into the hollow, marked by a thin orange ribbon tied to a low branch. It was his father's system, so that they could find the sets without hunting for them. Ethan had tied that ribbon himself early this morning. But the snare wasn’t where he’d placed it among the gooseberry bush that grew low and tangled at the base of one sapling. 

He stood there a moment, looking at the place where it should have been. The wire was gone. The saplings were undisturbed. No broken branches. No tangles in the underbrush the way an animal thrashing in a panic would leave it. 

Then he looked up.

The rabbit was there, hanging by a snared leg from the wire. But it wasn't the rabbit he'd left that morning. It had been — altered. Split open from chin to abdomen and the interior of it laid bare, the ribs spear wide like a blooming poppy, everything inside dark and glistening in the dim light. Around it the ground was blackened with blood, already old enough to have thickened and stopped moving. Whatever had done this hadn't been here moments ago. This had been done and left since his visit this morning.

The smell hit him then. A stale coating of rust and something else beneath it, something organic and sweet in the wrong way, like meat left out on a hot summers’ day.

He took a step back. His heel came down on a root and he staggered, caught himself on a nearby trunk as the rough bark raked at his palm.

He should go. He knew he should go. He should walk back the way he came, get on the path, get home. Coyote’s often leave their kill for later. Yeah, that’s what it was, a coyote, he told himself. How the coyote had managed to string it up over a branch, he had no idea. But right now, it was gone. He was fine, and he needed to leave.

He didn't go.

He stood there, staring at the opened rabbit, and he felt a certainty that he was not looking at something accidental. Not a predator's feeding. A predator would have taken the body or eaten it where it fell. This had been arranged. The rabbit, still in the snare, was the centre of something deliberate. A sign for him to read. 

A warning.

He thought about the clean absence of the wire where it should’ve been, the silence that had followed him down through the hollow, the way the air had seemed to thicken around him the closer he got, the way the creek had gone quiet at exactly the wrong moment. These things lined up in a way he didn't want them to. His mind kept trying to dismantle the line, kept reaching for the reasonable explanation. Coyote, he thought again, a fox, something that had dragged the snare up into the branches and left the rabbit there and by some coincidence of physics and tension, the line held. But that’s not what had happened. It held because whoever, or whatever had done this had rehung the snare. Had taken the time to dismantle it, open the animal, and string it back up. That was not instinct. That was not hunger. That was something that understood what a snare was for.

The wind lifted and moved through the canopy above him with a heavy breath, and in the same moment, the silence that had been merely uncomfortable became something physical. He felt it against his skin.

He turned to go, and something in the trees ahead of him made a sound.

It wasn't an animal sound, or it wasn't only that. It started low, somewhere at the lower extremities of what a human could hear, a vibration more than a sound, and then it shifted upward into something that suggested an animal without being one — a long, wet, gurgling pull of air. Not aggressive. More like a first full breath, awakening from a nightmare.

Ethan's body made the decision before his mind did. He ran.

He ran back the way he’d come, or at least where he thought he'd come from, and the trees seemed to close in around his every step, branches clawing for his face, the ground tilting and uneven under his feet. A sharp pain opened along his ribs where a broken branch caught him and dragged. He barely registered it. His lungs were burning and his mind was white and empty of everything except the next step.

He burst out of the hollow and onto what he thought was the path. He was in a part of the woods he didn't recognise — older trees, big ones, spruce and fir so tall that their tops were lost in the darkening fiery sky, their trunks close enough together that the spaces between them were barely wide enough to walk through. The single light over the step from his father's cabin, which should have been visible through the trees by now, was gone.

He stopped. Turned. Tried to locate himself by the slope of the ground, by the sound of the stream he knew ran south of the Corrigan’s, by anything.

Nothing was familiar. The years had changed it more than he'd accounted for. He'd been gone long enough for saplings to become trees, for clearings to close over, for the whole architecture of the place to shift. The landmarks he'd navigated by as a kid; the leaning birch at the top of the hill, the broad flat rock above where the creek had bent towards the pond, the pole line that’d been cut some thirty-odd years ago only to never erect a single mast. They were either gone, or buried so completely in new growth that he couldn't find them in the dark.

<i>You know this forest, </i>he told himself. <i>You've been through every part of it a thousand times. There's nothing here that wasn't here this morning.</i>

But the forest he’d known, the forest he’d mentally mapped and memorized throughout his childhood was no more. The trees looked wrong. They were too big. They were too close. And the bark on them — when he put his hand on the nearest trunk, shuddering and reaching for something solid — felt too cold, faintly so, like the bottom of a turned over stone. 

He walked quickly. He tried to walk in a straight line, keeping the slope to his left, certain that would bring him out above the valley, certain the cabin light would reappear. The trees did not open. If anything, they grew closer with each step. The slope seemed to shift each time he thought he’d adjusted his footing.

His lace caught a root and the ground came up fast and hard. His chin caught it before his palms, reopening the scar he’d had since five. He lay for a moment just breathing, face pressed into the cold wet earth, dead leaves against his lips, and he thought about his father. Not as a voice in his head with instructions, but as a person; the man who sat at the kitchen table after supper reading with his finger under the line, who always smelled faintly of woodsmoke and whiskey, who had never once laughed at Ethan for being afraid of something, only asked him what the thing actually was and waited for a real answer.

<i>What is it, Ethan? Describe it. Not the feeling — the thing.</I>

He pushed himself up.

He looked at where the trees should have had space between them and did not. He looked at the wall of trunks and undergrowth that stood in every direction he turned, seamless, unpassable, as though the forest had rearranged itself around his fall. He looked for the gaps he came through and couldn't find them.

He understood, on some level, that he wasn’t meant to.

---

He saw her. 

Her silhouette danced with a stillness much unlike the forest growth around them. He hadn't heard her. And the more he tried to focus on who, or what she is, she’d disappear into the blackness that grew between them. A woman, at the very least the shape of one, stood at the periphery where the trees closed together, standing so motionless that for a moment he thought she was another tree, another trick of shadow.

Then her eyes opened.

The light from them was amber like the light over the cabin door, but warm was the wrong word for it — it was the warm of embers, the remnants of a fire that had once blazed. The glow of her eyes had made everything around them darker, as if to focus Ethan directly on her very being, to prevent her from drifting in and out of his sight. In that light he saw that she was dressed in something white and shapeless, wearing nothing he could name, and that her face was turned toward him with an expression that he would never afterward be able to describe. 

She had known exactly where he would be.

Not in the way you might anticipate a person's movements, trace their habits, or reason your way to where they'd end up. But in the way a stone knows where it will land before it leaves your hand. The way a drain knows where the water will go.

There was no surprise, no adjustment, no moment of recognition crossing her face when she found him. She knew he would be there.

Ethan wanted to call to her, but fear clenched at his throat.

She gazed at him for a long moment. The amber light pulsed very slightly, like embers do. He noticed it did not illuminate the trees around her the way a lantern would have — it illuminated only him. As though she were looking at him in a stagelight, moments before the final curtain draw. 

He stood in silence. His mind pacing, offering nothing of use.

The light grew as if her amber gaze rekindled, flaring at pace toward him. Still, nothing had illuminated but him, and blindingly so. He clenched his eyes shut in recoil. 

As he opened them again, the light was gone.

The dark that followed was authoritative. The kind of darkness that presses at your core, that amplifies every other sense. Ethan could feel the cool breeze glide over every crease in his hands, he could smell the fresh decay of Earth as it molted beneath him. He heard, or thought he heard, the sound of her breathing, a slow emission of warmth that tickled the hair on his neck, and then nothing. Nothing for a moment that stretched; felt like eternity. Only breaking to the snap of a twig directly behind him.

He spun and there was nothing. Only the trees, the dark, the less than ordinary shape that the hollow had taken. He scanned what treeline he could see, hoping his eyes would adjust. They couldn’t. It was a darkness beyond what was biologically perceivable.

He stood there, his back to her. Unknownst of her very presence aside from the tingle of gooseflesh as it grew on his neck. He spun back and the full weight of her hit him.

He was on the ground before he understood he'd fallen. The air went out of him in a wheeze, his chest collapsing under her weight. His arms were pinned, though there was no sensation of hands holding them, only an inability to move them, as though the ground itself had taken its claim.

He struggled, but only sank deeper with every twitch and jerk.

Above him, the shape of her was darkness against darkness. He could see nothing of her face, nothing of her at all, only a silhouette defined by the faint and sourceless grey light that peeked through the canopy above. 

A sound reached him. Low. Close to his ear. Not a voice, but something that carried only the cadence of speech, the shape of a sentence with all the meaning removed. It moved across his skin like cold, delicate fingertips.

He did not scream. He tried, but he couldn’t. Whether his lungs had been depleted, or his vocal chords frozen with fear, he could not make the sound that he wanted. 

He felt the earth underneath him: cold, dense. The smell of it was everywhere, in his mouth and nostrils, mineral and rot, the smell of a place that had never seen the light of day, that is only discovered by those crawling through narrow passages into the depths of the Earth.

He felt the weight above him shift.

When her eyes opened again he was looking directly into them, close enough to see that the amber was not uniform — it moved, it had depth, there was something behind it the same way there is always something behind a window. Looking into it made him feel that time was not moving as it should. That in this very moment, ages passed and he would awaken from this nightmare only to find his loved ones in mourning.

Her expression held no anger. That was the thing he would come back to, later, in the days that would follow. Whatever she wanted from him — whatever this was — it was not emotional in a way he could comprehend. It was purposeful.

Whether the purpose was to harm, or simply to see him, he couldn't read her intent.

He searched her face for it, the way you scan a stranger’s at night when you’re unsure if what’s coming is a question or a threat. There was nothing to find. Not blankness — a blank expression was a readable thing, but a lack of anything he could read. She was not performing anything for him. She was simply regarding him with the full and total attention of something that had no other concern in the world, and the absence of any readable feeling in it was more frightening than anger or aggression. 

She held his gaze for what might have been several seconds or several years. Something passed through the amber light with fluctuation, some communication in an expression he couldn't trace.

Then she rose.

Not stood. Rose. Once there, then gone. The weight lifting without transition, the dark redistributing itself into an ordinary night, the bark that fortified the forest becoming trees again and not walls, the gaps between them reappearing as Ethan lay gasping in the wet earth.

The forest sounds returned. A gentle breeze. The flowing creek. A squirrel scurried through the branches above. A deafening rush of sound compared to what had been moments ago. 

He lay for a long time without moving.

When he finally sat up, he could see the lamp. His father's lamp. A warm amber glow. Nothing like her eyes. An ordinary amber that cast shadows through the trees as its flame danced on its mantle.

He stood on borrowed legs and walked toward the light.

He never told anyone what had happened that night. He told himself, in the weeks that followed, that he had panicked in the dark and frightened himself, concussed when he’d fallen and imagined the rest. It made sense. It fit his bill. 

Except that when he lay awake in the dark afterward, for weeks, for months, for years after that night, he could still feel that weight of her presence as if it never left. That uncomfortable patience. The sense that she had looked at him the way you look at something that you’ve decided to leave, for now.

Something you intend to return to.

He never felt, not once in all the years after, that she had finished with him. Instead, that she had left him the way he had left the rabbit — alive, ensnared, and somewhere she knew exactly how to find.


Something you intend to return to.

He never felt, not once in all the years after, that she had finished with him. Instead that she lingered in that forest, awaiting his return, for him to take one wrong step into her grasp.
